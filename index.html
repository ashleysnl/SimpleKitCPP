How this calculator works

A concise overview of the assumptions behind the estimates.

Methodology

  • The amount entered for age 65 is treated as the reference CPP benefit.
  • Age 60 and age 70 amounts are estimated using standard CPP timing adjustment factors.
  • Cumulative totals are projected from age 60 to your planning age using monthly cash flow steps.
  • Discounted values use your return assumption, net of inflation when shown as a real rate.

Important context

  • Actual CPP entitlement depends on your contribution history and Service Canada calculations.
  • This tool is educational and illustrative. It does not account for tax, GIS, OAS clawback, or personal health factors.
  • CPP timing should be reviewed alongside workplace pensions, RRSP/RRIF withdrawals, TFSA strategy, and cash flow needs.

FAQ

Short answers for the most common CPP timing questions.

Should I take CPP at 60, 65, or 70?

It depends on your cash flow needs, health, longevity expectations, and the rest of your retirement income. This calculator shows how the tradeoff changes under your assumptions.

What is the break-even age for delaying CPP?

It is the age when the total received from a later CPP start catches up to an earlier start. If that age is beyond your planning horizon, delaying may not pay off in total dollars under this scenario.

Does delaying CPP always pay off?

No. Delaying increases monthly guaranteed income, but the value of waiting depends on how long you expect to receive CPP and what alternative uses you have for early income.

Is CPP timing enough to decide my retirement plan?

No. CPP is only one part of retirement income. It should be considered together with pensions, government benefits, savings withdrawals, tax, and spending needs.

How accurate is this calculator?

It is directionally useful for planning, but it is not a Service Canada quote or regulated financial advice. Your actual entitlement and optimal strategy may differ.
